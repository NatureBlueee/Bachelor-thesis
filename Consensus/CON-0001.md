## Context
I am conducting a qualitative study on how AI literacy enables Generation Z employees to engage in upward influence behavior in Chinese organizations. 

My theoretical framework is based on Resource Dependence Theory (Pfeffer & Salancik, 1978; Tripathi, 2021), examining how AI literacy creates a resource-based power that enables employees to influence their supervisors.

Key constructs in my study:
- AI Literacy: AI knowledge, AI affectivity, AI thinking (Zhong et al., 2024)
- Perceived Resource Advantage: Employees' subjective perception of holding AI-related resources their supervisors need but lack
- Upward Influence Behavior: Idea generation, promotion, and implementation
- **Supervisor Openness**: The extent to which supervisors are receptive to subordinate input (MODERATOR in my model)

Currently, I have completed the literature review and methodology chapters, and I am preparing for qualitative interviews.

## Why I'm Asking
In my theoretical model, I propose that Supervisor Openness moderates the relationship between perceived resource advantage and upward influence behavior (P4). However, my supervisor raised an interesting hypothesis: "Supervisors with higher education and more technology experience are more likely to be receptive to AI-related suggestions from younger employees."

I need to examine what the literature says about the **antecedents of supervisor openness**. Specifically:
1. Does educational background predict openness?
2. Does prior technology experience predict openness?
3. Does learning goal orientation (growth mindset) predict openness?

I want to know if this is an established finding or if it's a gap I should explore in my interviews.

## My Question
What factors have been empirically identified as antecedents of supervisor openness to subordinate input (employee voice, creative ideas, upward influence attempts)? 

Specifically, I'm looking for studies that examine whether supervisors' educational background, prior technology/digital experience, or learning goal orientation predict their receptiveness to ideas from lower-status or younger employees.

I'm also open to discovering dimensions I'm not aware of. If there are other antecedents (e.g., humble leadership, psychological safety, status threat) that the literature discusses, please include them.

## Output Requirements
Please act as an experienced organizational behavior researcher. I need **granular details**, not just conclusions:

- **Study details**: Sample size, country/industry, methodology (survey/interview/experiment)
- **Measurement**: How was "openness" measured? What scales? How many items?
- **Effect sizes**: Specific coefficients (Î², r) if quantitative
- **Mechanisms**: WHY does this antecedent affect openness? What's the theoretical logic?
- **Boundary conditions**: When does the effect hold or not?

Avoid statements like "X is positively related to Y" without the supporting details.

## Why I'm Asking
My study is about younger employees (Gen Z) with AI skills influencing older supervisors. This is conceptually similar to "reverse mentoring" where knowledge flows from junior to senior. I want to understand what makes senior employees (in my case, supervisors) successfully learn from juniors.

## My Question
In empirical studies of reverse mentoring, what characteristics of senior employees (mentees) predicted their successful learning from younger mentors?

I'm specifically interested in:
1. Studies conducted in technology/AI/digital skills domains
2. The role of learning orientation or growth mindset of the mentee
3. How "successful learning" was operationalized and measured
4. Whether ego threat or status concerns moderated the effect

## Output Requirements
I need **granular details**, not just conclusions:

- Study details: sample size, industry/country, methodology
- Measurement: how was "successful learning" operationalized?
- Effect sizes: specific coefficients if quantitative
- Mechanisms: WHY do certain characteristics predict success?
- Boundary conditions: when does the effect hold or not?
